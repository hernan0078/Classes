Nia's Peacock Tail

1    Nia had to cut a hole in her favorite jeans to accommodate the wondrous tail with which she’d awaken that morning. The pearly green feathers fanned out behind and above her, a sheltering umbrella. With her arms outstretched, Nia could barely touch the wide-open purple eyes that dotted the feathers’ tips. She found that with some concentration, she could relax the tail so that it dragged behind her on the floor like the train of a royal gown, and by this means, she was able to exit her bedroom door and parade grandly down the hall with poise and majesty to greet her subjects.

2    All her thirteen years, Nia had been waiting for something like this to happen, for the shimmering glory within her to burst forth and show itself at last to the world. (Although, to be honest, she hadn't quite imagined this.) But now, everyone could see. Never again when she entered a room could anyone possibly say, with a hint of disappointment, "Oh, it's just Nia."

3    In the kitchen, where her family was seated for breakfast, Nia placed her hands on her hips and cleared her throat. With a soft rustle, the tail expanded, its colors catching the morning sunlight. She smiled, anticipating wild admiration, but her mother, father, and brother all looked at her in mild annoyance, as though she’d paraded into the room blasting a tuba.

4    "Well, that's an interesting choice," her mother said, as if the tail were just another fashion accessory. Her mother’s eyes quickly went to the floor. Tiny feathers, shaken free from the tail, tumbled around under the table like the debris of a pillow fight. "Nia, watch what you're doing," her mother scolded as she went for the broom.

5    "Sorry," Nia muttered, and the tail began to droop.

6    Her father rose from the table and, planting a quick kiss on Nia’s head before rushing out the door, said, "Don't forget to take out the trash today, hon." The tail drooped even more.

7    Nia's brother plucked a feather out of his cereal and flicked it away, wrinkling his nose. "Do you still have time to help me with my homework?" he said.

8    "I guess so." Nia let the tail drop clumsily to the floor and joined her brother at the table.

9    "We're supposed to write a poem about wintertime," he said, handing her a sheet of paper on which he’d scrawled a few lines. "But you’re so much better at that stuff than I am. You're the one with the creative spirit or whatever."

10    "I am?" Nia said, and the tail twitched; its purple eyes blinked open.

11    "Everyone says so," her brother said. "It's so totally obvious."

In this science fiction passage, Captain Dan Barstow embarks on the first “flight to infinity,” reporting on his journey to his colonel. This story was written in 1954, when reaching an altitude of 1,000 miles, along with other flying accomplishments we take for granted or think of as nothing today, was regarded by the writer as an extraordinary feat.

from “Shipwreck in the Sky”

by Eando Binder

1

The flight was listed at GHQ as Project Songbird. It was sponsored by the Space Medicine Labs of the U.S. Air Force. And its pilot was Captain Dan Barstow.

2

A hand-picked man, Dan Barstow, chosen for the AF’s most important project of the year because he and his VX-3 had already broken all previous records set by hordes of V-2s, Navy Aerobees and anything else that flew the skyways.

3

Dan Barstow, first man to cross the sea of air and sight open, unlimited space. Pioneer flight to infinity. He grinned and hummed to himself as he settled down for the long jaunt. Too busy to be either thrilled or scared he considered the thirty-seven instruments he’d have to read, the twice that many records to keep, and the miles of camera film to run. He had been hand-picked and thoroughly conditioned to take it all without more than a ten percent increase in his pulse rate. So he worked as matter-of-factly as if he were down in the Gs Centrifuge1 of the Space Medicine Labs where he had been schooled for this trip for months.

4

He kept up a running fire of oral reports through his helmet radio, down to Rough Rock and his CO2 . . . .

5

Dan couldn’t help wincing when the meteors peppered down past. The “flak” of space. Below he could see the meteors flare up brightly as they hit the atmosphere. Most of those near his position were small, none bigger than a baseball, and Dan took comfort in the fact that his rocket was small too, in the immensity around him. A direct hit would be sheer bad luck, but the good old law of averages was on his side. . . .

6

“Cosmic rays, sir? Sure, the reading shot up double on the Geiger3 . . . huh? Naw, I don’t feel a thing . . . like Doc Baird suspected, we invented a lot of [tales] in advance, before going into space. I feel fine, so you can put down cosmic ray intensity as a Boogey Man4 . . . . What’s that? Yeah, yeah, sir, the stars shine without winking up here. What else? . . . Space is inky black—no deep purples or . . . more-than-blacks like some jetted-up writers dreamed up—just plain old ordinary dead black. Earth, sir? . . . Well, it does look dish-shaped from up here, concave5 . . . . Sure, I can see all the way to Europe and—say! Here’s something unexpected. I can see that hurricane off the coast of Florida. . . . You said it, sir! Once we install permanent space stations up here it will be easy to spot typhoons, volcano eruptions, tidal waves, earthquakes, what have you, the moment they start. If you ask me, with a good telescope you could even spot forest fires the minute they broke out. . . .”

7

Dan broke off and almost retched as his stomach turned a flip-flop to end all flip-flops. The VX-3 had reached the peak of its trajectory6 at over 1000 miles altitude and now turned down, lazily at first. He gulped oxygen from the emergency tube at his lips and felt better.

8

“Turning back on schedule, Rough Rock. Peak altitude 1037 miles. Everything fine, no danger. This was all a cinch. . . . HEY! Wait. . . . Something not in the books has popped up . . . stand by!”

9

Dan had felt the rocket swing a bit, strangely, as if gripped by a strong force. Instead of falling directly down toward Earth with a slight pitch, it slanted sideways and spun on its long axis. And then Dan saw what it was. . . .

10

Beneath, intercepting his trajectory, coming around fast over the curvature of Earth, was a tiny black worldlet, 998 miles above Earth. It might be an enormous meteor, but Dan felt he was right the first time. For it wasn’t falling like a meteor but swinging parallel to Earth’s surface on even keel.

11

He stared at the unexpected discovery, as amazed as if it were a fire-breathing dragon out of legend. For it was, actually, he realized in swift, stunned comprehension, more amazing than any legend. . . .
